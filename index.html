The brief

This is the working plan, not a brochure. Every day has a primary timeline, a branch plan if something runs long or closes, and a rain line for a wet day. The three ancestral stops are protected anchors ( copper markers) and the schedule is built so nothing crowds them out.

One correction baked into this plan. Your draft routed you to Hurdal for a great-grandfather, but your records put Erick Amundsen Dihle in Gran, Hadeland. That is a better stop and it sits right on the road north, so Hurdal is out and Gran (Granavollen) is in, home to Norway's medieval Sister Churches and the Hadeland open-air museum.
What this trip is really about

Three threads run the whole way: heritage (three ancestors who all emigrated to North Dakota, from three different valleys), history & architecture (your lane: fortresses, stave and stone churches, resistance museums), and kid fuel (LEGOLAND, a real Olympic bobsled run, science centers, fjord hikes). A serious PhD-scouting sub-mission runs through Copenhagen and Oslo.

Tempo & rules of the road

Drive cap: 12 h comfortable, 15 h possible. Two longest days are D1 and D13.

Rotation: you and Sylvia split the wheel; both hold IDP.

Pattern: alternate high-demand and low-demand days, no pure rest days.

Flex: 16 Aug is a buffer; use it only to split the return if D13–D15 feel heavy.

Home by: ~2100 on 15 Aug.

Budget reality, up front. $5,000 for five people across 15 days in Scandinavia is tight. It is reachable on the Lean track (camp more, cook nearly every meal, one big paid attraction), but the comfortable version with hotels most nights and regular restaurants lands closer to $6,300. The Budget section gives you both columns and the exact levers.

Roots — the three ancestors

Three great-grandparents, three valleys, one destination: all of them left for North Dakota. The trip touches each home parish. The single biggest thing you can do to make these visits land is two to three hours in the Norwegian archives before you leave, so you walk into the right churchyard and onto the right farm, not an approximation.

Martha Westerheim (Sand)

Born 16 Sep 1887 · Westerheim, Ulvik, Hardanger

Died 18 Jan 1993 · New Town, North Dakota

Visit Ulvik kyrkje (Day 11) + Vesterheim farm

Ulvik church is open daily through 20 Aug and the verger gives tours, so your timing is ideal. "Westerheim/Vesterheim" is the farm name; pin its exact location from the registers + Kartverket so you can stand on the ground she was born on. Her married name "Sand" suggests the Sand family was local too, worth a second surname search.

Erick Amundsen Dihle

Born 13 May 1881 · Gran, Hadeland

Died 18 Apr 1950 · Burke County, North Dakota

Visit Søsterkirkene, Granavollen (Day 8)

"Dihle/Dæhli" is the farm; "Amundsen" means his father was Amund. The Sister Churches were the Hadeland parish churches, so this is almost certainly where he was baptized. The Hadeland Folkemuseum holds a photo/document archive for the district, a strong on-site backstop if the farm isn't fully pinned.

Tollef Andersen Bratlien

Born 9 Aug 1871 · Torpa, Nordre Land

Died 12 Aug 1948 · Noonan, Divide County, North Dakota

Visit Åmot Church (primary) / Lunde Church (Day 9)

"Bratlien" is the farm; "Andersen" means his father was Anders. Torpa parish has two churches, Åmot (main) and Lunde (Vest-Torpa), so the 1871 baptism record is what tells you which churchyard to stand in. Note he was born 9 Aug, so you'll be in his valley within a day of his birthday.

The thread

All three landed in North Dakota (New Town, Burke County, Divide County), within a roughly 150-mile arc of the northwest corner of the state. That is the classic late-1800s Norwegian-American settlement story: three Norwegian valleys feeding one prairie county-cluster a generation later. Worth telling the kids as you go, it makes the abstract concrete.

Advance archive work — do this in June

Everything below is free. Budget one evening per ancestor.

  1. Create a free account at Digitalarkivet (digitalarkivet.no), the Norwegian National Archives' digital site. It holds scanned parish registers (kirkebøker), censuses, and emigration records.
  2. Pull each baptism record (dåp): Ulvik 1887, Gran 1881, Torpa 1871. These confirm the exact farm, the parents' names, and, for Torpa, which church. This single step resolves the Åmot-vs-Lunde question.
  3. Pull the censuses (folketelling 1875, 1891, 1900, 1910). These place each family on their named farm and list siblings, the ones who didn't emigrate are your trail to living relatives.
  4. Pull the emigration records (the "utflytta" column in the church book, or the emigrant protocols) to confirm departure year and port.
  5. Locate each farm on Norgeskart / Kartverket (the national map authority). Drop a pin and save offline coordinates so you can navigate straight there.
  6. Order or read the bygdebøker (local farm-and-family history books): "Hadeland: Bygdenes historie" for Gran, "Boka om Land" for Nordre Land/Torpa, and the Ulvik gards- og ættesoge for Ulvik. Many volumes are digitized at the National Library (nb.no); the rest the local historielag can help with.
  7. Email the parish offices to confirm the grave and arrange access (template below). Email the local historical societies (historielag) and the museums (Hadeland Folkemuseum, Hardanger Folkemuseum) about the families.
Finding living relatives. From the 1900/1910 census, note the siblings who stayed and the farm they were on. PhD scouting — microbiology & genetics

You said this is serious and you're looking for mentors, not a campus selfie. A walk-through tells you almost nothing; a 30-minute conversation with a principal investigator tells you everything. The whole value of the Copenhagen and Oslo campus windows depends on emailing two or three PIs in June to set up informal meetings during your visit dates.

Timing is the catch. Late July is the Scandinavian academic holiday (fellesferie); many faculty return in early-to-mid August. Your Copenhagen (4 Aug) and Oslo (7 Aug) windows fall just as people come back, which is workable, but only if you've written ahead. Some PIs will still be away, so contact more than one per city.

Two tracks for a family of five, 14 nights. Lean hits your $5,000 target but only with real discipline: camp more, cook nearly every meal, and treat LEGOLAND as the one big paid attraction. The levers, biggest first

1. Lodging is the single biggest swing. Each hotel night you convert to a camping cabin saves ~$60–100.

2. Food. Restaurants in Norway run $25–40 per main. Cooking in cabins and packing hotel-breakfast lunches is worth ~$25/day.

3. Ulvik tier. Brakanes Hotel (fjord-front) vs. a campsite cabin is ~$100/night × 3 = $300.

4. Activities. Most of your history stops (fortress grounds, churches, Vøringsfossen, Vigeland) are free or cheap. The paid temptations are LEGOLAND, Hunderfossen, the wheelbob, and Tivoli.

Currencies & cards

4 currencies: EUR (Germany), DKK (Denmark), SEK (Sweden), NOK (Norway). None of the Nordics use the euro.

Go cashless. Scandinavia runs on cards/Apple Pay almost everywhere, including tiny farm shops. You barely need cash.

Use a no-foreign-transaction-fee card and decline "pay in dollars/euros" prompts (always choose the local currency to avoid bad conversion).

Tolls are billed to the plate, so set up the payment accounts (next section) before you go rather than fumbling cash at booths.

If $5,000 is a hard ceiling, the realistic plan is: camp/cabin every night you're not in a city, cook every breakfast and lunch plus most dinners, do LEGOLAND and exactly one Lillehammer paid activity (wheelbob or Hunderfossen), and lean on the free history. That lands near $4,900–5,100. If you can flex to ~$6,000, take the breakfast hotels in the four cities and stop sweating every meal.

Logistics & appendices

Tolls & payment setup (do before departure)

Danish bridges

Great Belt (Storebælt): tolled eastbound only (~€36 car). Free westbound, so your return crossing is free.

Øresund (to/from Sweden): ~€55–60, charged both directions; you cross it twice. Booth on the Swedish side.

Set up BroBizz or number-plate payment for the discount and to skip cash lanes. Note both bridges measure your vehicle's length and height, the F-150 sits near the car/large-vehicle line, so don't be surprised by a slightly higher rate.

Norway & Sweden

Norway (AutoPASS): fully automatic plate-based tolls, no booths. Register the F-150 with EPASS24 before the trip so you're billed correctly as a foreign vehicle.

Gothenburg: small weekday congestion tax (trängselskatt), auto-billed via plate; a few dollars total.

Germany: no car tolls (Autobahn is free for passenger vehicles).

Practical notes

Connectivity & emergencies

Emergency: 112 works across Germany, Denmark, Sweden, and Norway.

Phone: EU roaming covers Germany/Denmark/Sweden. Norway is EEA and most EU plans include it, confirm with your carrier, or drop in a cheap eSIM (e.g. a Nordic/EU data eSIM) for the Norway week.

Offline: download Google Maps offline areas for Hardangervidda and the Norwegian valleys, coverage thins on the plateau.

The truck

Low-emission zones: Denmark (Copenhagen, Aarhus, etc.) and some Swedish/German cities have LEZ rules aimed mainly at older diesels; a 2024 gas hybrid is fine, but you may need to register the plate for the Danish/Swedish systems, check before you cross.

Size: the F-150 is wide for old town centers and tight European parking, park at the edge and walk/transit in cities (especially Copenhagen and Oslo).

Fuel: cheapest in Germany; top off before Denmark and again before Norway, which is the priciest.
